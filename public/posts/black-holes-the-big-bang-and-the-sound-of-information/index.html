3.3 What standard cosmology actually says about the beginning#

In the standard Big Bang model:

  • If you run Einstein’s equations backward in time, the universe gets hotter, denser, and “smaller.”
  • The equations predict that, at a finite time in the past, the density becomes infinite and the size of space shrinks to zero. This is the Big Bang singularity.

However, almost everyone agrees the singularity is not literally a real physical point:

  • It’s better seen as a sign that our current equations (general relativity + ordinary quantum field theory) break down at extremely high densities and tiny scales.
  • To understand what really happens at those scales, we need a consistent theory of quantum gravity. We don’t have the final version of that yet.

Because of this, many physicists explore models where:

  • The singularity is replaced by a bounce: a previous contracting phase reaches a minimum size and then re‑expands.
  • Or where the very notion of “before the Big Bang” becomes more subtle (e.g., time itself emerges from a more fundamental, non‑time‑like structure).

So your π analogy—getting ever closer to something without an actual, well‑defined “last step”—captures the feel of these proposals quite well. In some models, there really is no absolute “first moment,” only some limiting regime where our familiar picture of time and space stops applying and a different description takes over.

3.4 Does the universe have a central black hole “core”?#

Here is where standard cosmology pushes back.

On large scales, observations show that:

  • The universe is homogeneous (roughly the same everywhere, when you average over large enough distances).
  • It is also isotropic (looks the same in every direction).

That means:

There is no special central point inside our universe that everything is expanding away from.

Instead, space itself is expanding. A common analogy is the surface of a balloon with dots on it: as the balloon inflates, every dot sees all other dots moving away, and there is no special dot at the “center” of the expansion on the surface itself.

So the idea that there is a literal central black hole within our universe that powers the expansion doesn’t fit the data or the standard equations that describe cosmic expansion.

3.5 A universe inside a black hole#

There is a twist that sounds much more like your picture:

Some researchers have proposed black hole cosmology, where:

  • Our entire universe is actually the interior of a black hole that formed in some larger “parent” universe.
  • In these scenarios, matter collapsing into that black hole avoids forming a true singularity and instead undergoes a bounce.
  • After the bounce, it expands again—on the inside—as a new universe, which would look to observers inside it like a Big Bang.

In that picture:

  • There is a black hole, but we are on the inside of it.
  • The Big Bang is closely related to a black hole collapse and bounce, rather than being an unrelated one‑off event.
  • Each black hole in the parent universe might spawn its own “baby universe” with its own laws and constants.

This is conceptually very close to your idea of a black-hole‑like core emitting an expanding universe, but it avoids having a “center” within our observable universe, which would conflict with observations.

3.6 What do black holes really do with matter?#

In your picture, black holes:

  • Suck everything in through the event horizon.
  • Then they re‑emit what they absorbed through radiation until they disappear.

Standard physics says:

  • Classically (in pure general relativity), anything that crosses the event horizon is gone from our universe’s observable region; it cannot get back out.
  • Quantum mechanically, black holes are predicted to emit Hawking radiation—a very faint radiation that slowly causes them to lose mass and eventually evaporate.
  • For big astrophysical black holes, this evaporation takes an absurdly long time, far longer than the current age of the universe.

So, in principle, a black hole can turn its mass into radiation. Whether all the detailed information about what fell in is preserved in that radiation is exactly what the information paradox is about.

There are also speculative proposals in which:

  • The collapse into a black hole and its eventual evaporation or bounce leads to the creation of a new universe.
  • Black holes are not final “dead ends” but transitions in a larger multiverse structure.

Again, this resonates with your “it’s like a circle” idea: compress → bounce / radiate → expand.

3.7 The black hole information paradox in simple terms#

Now let’s connect to your second idea: using “sound” or ripples to help solve the information paradox.

Very roughly, the information paradox goes like this:

  1. Black holes in classical general relativity

    • Are fully described by only a few macroscopic parameters: mass, charge, and spin (“no-hair theorem”).
    • Once something crosses the event horizon, no signals from it can escape to the outside world.

  2. Hawking radiation in semiclassical quantum theory

    • Hawking showed that black holes emit radiation with a thermal (random-looking) spectrum.
    • In his original calculation, this radiation depends only on the macroscopic parameters of the black hole, not on the detailed quantum state of what fell in.

  3. The problem

    • Suppose you start with some very specific quantum state (a detailed configuration of particles), let it collapse into a black hole, and then let the black hole fully evaporate into Hawking radiation.
    • If that radiation is purely thermal and featureless, many different initial states would lead to exactly the same final state.
    • That would mean the original information about what fell in is destroyed.

But in quantum mechanics, the evolution of a closed system is supposed to be unitary: information is never truly lost; it can be scrambled and spread out, but not destroyed. The clash between Hawking’s thermal radiation and quantum unitarity is the black hole information paradox.

Modern research (especially involving holography and related ideas) strongly hints that:

  • Black hole evaporation is in fact unitary.
  • The Hawking radiation is not perfectly thermal; it contains extremely subtle correlations that encode the information about what fell in.
  • Our semiclassical calculation is too crude and misses these correlations.

But the exact, fully realistic story remains an active area of research.

What we actually “hear” from black holes#

In reality:

  • When two black holes orbit each other and merge, they emit gravitational waves—ripples in spacetime itself.
  • Detectors like LIGO pick up these waves as tiny stretches and squeezes of space, and we then convert the signal into audio to “hear” it as a chirp.
  • The waveform does carry information: from it we can infer the masses, spins, and orbit of the black holes that merged, and the properties of the final black hole.

So, yes: ripples emitted by black hole systems do carry information about the matter and motion involved.

Why this doesn’t quite solve the paradox#

However, the information paradox is stricter:

  • It cares about all the microscopic quantum information, not just some coarse properties.
  • Gravitational waves and other classical or semiclassical emissions encode things like masses, spins, orbital parameters, etc. They do not (at least in our usual description) encode the full quantum state of every particle that ever fell in.
  • The tricky part of the paradox is what happens to the information after matter has crossed the event horizon, when no further classical signals can get out.

So, even if we include:

  • All the light, neutrinos, and gravitational waves emitted during the collapse and before horizon formation, there is still a gap: what about the quantum information that ends up inside and then seems to vanish when the black hole evaporates?

That is the part that “sound” in the broad sense doesn’t fully address.

Where your idea does match modern thinking#

Where your intuition lines up nicely with current ideas is here:

  • Everything that escapes—Hawking radiation, gravitational waves, any other form of radiation—must be treated as part of one big output channel for information.
  • If quantum gravity is unitary, then all these channels together must, in principle, carry all the information about what fell in.
  • The difference is that physicists talk about quantum fields and entanglement and subtle correlations, rather than a special status for “sound.” But the spirit—ripples as carriers of information—is the same.

So your idea is not a formal solution to the paradox, but it is philosophically close to how many physicists now think: black holes do not destroy information; they hide and scramble it in complicated ways in the radiation and correlations that eventually leak out.

  1. The Mathematical Paradox of the “Dot”: Continuum vs. Singularity

The concept of the Big Bang singularity relies on the assumption that spacetime is a continuous manifold—a smooth fabric that can be subdivided infinitely. This assumption leads directly to the “dot” model, where the scale factor of the universe $a(t)$ approaches zero as time $t$ approaches zero. However, as the user correctly identifies, this mathematical abstraction clashes with physical reality.

2.1 The Geometry of Infinite Density

In General Relativity, the singularity is not merely a point in space; it is a boundary of spacetime itself. According to the Penrose-Hawking singularity theorems, if gravity is always attractive and the universe contains sufficient matter, the curvature of spacetime must have been infinite in the finite past.1 At this “dot,” the density $\rho$ diverges:

$$\lim_{t \to 0} \rho(t) = \infty$$

This divergence is physically problematic because it implies an infinite amount of information and energy confined to a zero-volume region. In the user’s terminology, the “dot” represents a breakdown of causal structure. If the universe were truly a dot, the entropy would be zero, and the “arrow of time” would cease to exist. However, the user’s intuition suggests that “it’s not a dot.” This aligns with modern criticism of the singularity as a “coordinate artifact” or a sign that the theory of General Relativity is incomplete.2 Just as the behavior of a fluid breaks down when one analyzes it at the level of individual atoms, the smooth geometry of Einstein’s spacetime breaks down at the scale of the “dot.”

2.2 The “Pi” Analogy: Zeno’s Paradox and the Asymptotic Past

The user introduces a powerful analogy: the calculation of $\pi$. $\pi$ is an irrational, transcendental number with an infinite sequence of non-repeating digits. One can calculate $\pi$ with increasing precision—3.14, 3.141, 3.1415—getting “closer and closer” to the true value, yet never completing the sequence.

This analogy maps directly onto Zeno’s Paradox of Motion applied to cosmic time. If time is a continuum (like the real number line), then the interval between $t=1$ second and $t=0$ contains an infinite number of instants. To reach the “beginning,” one must traverse half the distance, then half of that, ad infinitum.4

Logarithmic Divergence: Physicist Charles Misner formalized this “Pi Paradox” in cosmology. He proposed that the “age” of the universe should not be measured in linear time $t$, but in a logarithmic time variable $\Omega = -\ln(t)$. As $t \to 0$, $\Omega \to \infty$. In this view, the universe has existed for an infinite number of “events” or processing steps, even if linear time appears finite. Just as the digits of $\pi$ stretch to infinity, the physical history of the universe stretches back through infinite epochs of becoming.4

The Asymptotic Barrier: The user argues, “you can indefinitely go closer and closer… but you can actually never reach it.” This is consistent with models where the Big Bang is an asymptotic limit. In “Emergent Gravity” scenarios, the “beginning” is akin to Absolute Zero temperature—one can approach it asymptotically, but thermodynamics prevents physically reaching the “zero state.”

2.3 Refutation of the “Dot” via Information Theory

If the universe were a true singularity (“dot”), it would possess zero entropy and zero information capacity. However, the universe we observe contains vast amounts of information. The “Pi” analogy reinforces the impossibility of the “dot”: $\pi$ contains infinite information (since it never repeats). Compressing an infinite complexity (like $\pi$) into a zero-dimensional point creates a logical contradiction. Thus, the “beginning” cannot be a simple point; it must be a structure capable of holding the “seed” information of the cosmos.   1. Black Hole Cosmology: The Interior Solution

The user moves from the geometry of the “square” to the architecture of the cosmos: “The core of the universe is actually something like a black hole… radiation basically becomes the expanding universe.” This intuitive leap corresponds to Black Hole Cosmology (BHC), a rigorous theoretical framework where the observable universe is the interior of a black hole residing in a larger parent universe.

4.1 The Schwarzschild-Hubble Coincidence

The strongest evidence for BHC is a startling numerical coincidence in our universe’s parameters.

Hubble Radius ($R_H$): The distance to the edge of the observable universe is approximately $1.3 \times 10^{26}$ meters.

Mass of the Universe ($M_U$): Based on observed density, the mass is approximately $10^{53}$ kg.

Schwarzschild Radius ($R_S$): The radius of a black hole with mass $M_U$ is calculated as $R_S = \frac{2GM_U}{c^2}$.

When one performs this calculation, $R_S \approx R_H$. The radius of our universe is almost exactly equal to the radius of a black hole with the same mass. This suggests that the universe satisfies the condition for being a black hole.18 To an observer outside our universe (in the “Parent” universe), our cosmos would appear as a static Schwarzschild black hole. To us on the inside, the geometry appears as an expanding Friedmann-Lemaître-Robertson-Walker (FLRW) metric.

4.2 Popławski’s Torsion and the Bounce Mechanism

The user states: “Because the black hole is not a still and death thing, it’s continually expanding… you can basically infinite long go more in a more dense black hole.”

Standard General Relativity predicts that matter inside a black hole hits the singularity and vanishes. However, Einstein-Cartan-Sciama-Kibble (ECSK) gravity—which adds the property of spin and torsion to spacetime—predicts a different outcome that matches the user’s “continually expanding” description.

Torsion Repulsion: Fermions (electrons, quarks) have intrinsic spin. At normal densities, this spin is negligible. But at the extreme densities inside a black hole (approaching the Planck scale/“square” limit), the spins align and generate spacetime torsion. This torsion acts as a powerful repulsive force, counteracting gravity.20

The Non-Singular Bounce: Torsion prevents the formation of the “dot” singularity. The collapsing matter from the parent universe is compressed to a minimum finite radius and then undergoes a bounce.
